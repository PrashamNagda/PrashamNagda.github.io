Why Tesla Robotaxis Switched to ClickHouse for 10ms Telemetry Analytics

Data Engineering 12 min read January 27, 2026

Listen, if you're still trying to pipe high-frequency sensor data into a standard relational database, you're not just behind—you're basically trying to win a Formula 1 race in a rickshaw.

Back at NMIMS Mumbai, I was knee-deep in hardware, debugging drivetrain faults for Drishti Works beach robots. The real headache? We had a 40% fault rate because we couldn't see the data fast enough to stop a failure before it happened. We were flying blind. Fast forward to my time at Northeastern, and the scale has shifted from a few robots to millions of devices.

In 2026, the industry standard for this kind of "holy crap, that's a lot of data" problem isn't Postgres or even a standard Snowflake setup. It's ClickHouse. Here's why the big players like Tesla are ditching traditional row-based storage for columnar speed, and how you can build a pipeline that recruiters will actually respect.

The 1TB/Hour Problem: Why Postgres Chokes

Most people start with Postgres because it's reliable. But when you're dealing with Tesla-level telemetry—think GPS, battery thermals, LiDAR state, and drivetrain torque—you're hitting millions of rows per second.

Postgres is row-oriented. To find the average battery temp of every Model 3 in Boston over the last hour, it has to scan every single row and every single column in those rows. It's slow. It's expensive. It's a bottleneck.

ClickHouse is column-oriented. If you only need "Temperature" and "Timestamp," it only reads those two columns. It ignores the rest of the metadata. On the HackerNews frontpage, the consensus is clear: for OLAP (Online Analytical Processing) at the edge, if you aren't using a columnar store, you're burning money on compute.

The Real-World Stack: Tinybird + ClickHouse + Kafka

In 2026, nobody is manually writing INSERT statements for telemetry. The architecture shipping at top-tier startups looks like this:

  1. The Edge (The "NMIMS" Layer): ESP32-S3 or Coral TPUs running Zephyr RTOS stream compact Protobuf messages over MQTT.
  2. The Pipeline: A Kafka or Redpanda cluster ingests the firehose.
  3. The Storage: ClickHouse consumes directly from Kafka via the Kafka Storage Engine. No intermediate "glue" code that breaks at 3 AM.
  4. The API: Tinybird sits on top of ClickHouse, turning SQL queries into high-performance REST APIs in seconds.

When I built the duplicate detection dashboard at SynapZen, the real win wasn't just the ML model; it was the Streamlit + PostgreSQL speed. But if I had 11.5 million records instead of 11.5k, Postgres would have folded. Switching to a ClickHouse-backed engine is how you go from "student project" to "enterprise-grade."

The "Secret Sauce": MergeTree Engines

If a recruiter asks how you handle high-frequency inserts, don't say "I just save it." Mention the MergeTree engine family.

ClickHouse doesn't update rows. It writes parts and merges them in the background. It's designed for immutable, timestamped data. This is exactly what I needed back when I was doing solar power management—writing data as it happens and letting the engine handle the heavy lifting.

Pro Tip: In 2026, use ReplicatedMergeTree with ClickHouse Keeper. It's the "total jugaad" for high availability without the complexity of Zookeeper.

Implementation: How to Build This for Your Portfolio

Don't just talk about it—ship it. Here is the exact roadmap I'm using to level up my own portfolio:

  • Step 1: The Mock Firehose. Use a Python script to mimic 100 "robots" sending JSON telemetry to a Kafka topic.
  • Step 2: The Direct Consume. Set up a ClickHouse table with the ENGINE = Kafka setting. Let ClickHouse pull the data itself.
  • Step 3: The Aggregate. Create a Materialized View. This is the 2026 way to pre-calculate averages (like my 18% solar efficiency metrics) so your dashboard loads in 10ms, not 10 seconds.
  • Step 4: The Frontend. Connect it to Grafana or Evidence.dev.

Final Real-Talk

Recruiters at places like Tesla or Amazon aren't looking for people who can write a SELECT *. They want engineers who understand Data Gravity. They want to know you can handle the "Real-time Edge" without the system crashing.

Moving from Mumbai to Boston taught me one thing: the tools change, but the hustle to optimize remains the same. Stop building batch pipelines for a real-time world. Learn ClickHouse.

NMIMS taught me hardware. Northeastern taught me scale. ClickHouse is how I bridge them.
